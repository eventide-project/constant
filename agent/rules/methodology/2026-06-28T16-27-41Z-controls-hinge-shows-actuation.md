# At the controls hinge, show the controls with the actuation and the assertion (the full test body, no contexts)

When presenting the controls hinge (hinge 3 of the cycle), show the **controls, the actuation, and the assertion together** — the full test body minus its nested contexts — not the controls alone. By hinge 3 both the actuation (hinge 1) and the assertion (hinge 2) are settled; the controls only make sense in terms of how the call consumes them and what the assertion then checks.

**Why:** Controls in isolation are inert — a top-level constant, an inner name, a namespace name say nothing about whether they exercise the outcome. Their correctness is a function of how the actuation uses them (which argument each becomes) *and* what the assertion examines about the result. Showing the controls together with the actuation and the assertion puts the judgment where it belongs: do *these* controls, fed into *this* call, produce a result *this* assertion verifies — and does the whole thing exercise the outcome? Showing the controls alone forces the human to reconstruct that linkage mentally.

**How to apply:** At hinge 3, render the control declarations, then the actuation (bound to its explaining variable), then the assertion's explaining variables and the `test` block — the complete test body, with the nested `context` blocks omitted (those were shown at the actuation hinge and are not repeated). The actuation and assertion were settled at earlier hinges; here they are shown for context, not re-decided. Related: the hinge-cycle rule, the actuation-hinge-shows-contexts rule, the assertion-hinge-shows-actuation rule, and the test-block-is-assertion-only rule.
